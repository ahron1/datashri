Rate of growth of COVID-19 cases in India

Date: 27 March 2020
Last update: 08 April 2020

By 8 April 2020, India has more than 5000 COVID19 infections reported. As of 02 April, the number of reported COVID19 infections in India had crossed 2000. About 3 weeks back, this number was about 200. Between 01 March and 02 April, the number of infections have doubled roughly every 4-5 days. In the days between April 5 - April 8, the growth somewhat slowed down.

This is typical in epidemics - the number of infections grows exponentially before starting to gradually plateau. This is one of the reasons it is common to model the number of infections with a sigmoid curve (as illustrated below)

As can be seen in the illustration above, sigmoidal curves can have different degrees of steepness and can plateau off at various levels too. The varying levels of steepness and levels of plateauing off are modelled by the parameters of the sigmoid curve - with each set of parameters representing a certain steepness and plateau. It is still too early to match the COVID19 infections in India with a specific set of parameters. Since the infection is still in its early stages, the future rate of growth is still unknown.

Left entirely unchecked, it can rise exponentially till it has infected everyone. If measures are taken to curb the spread of the infection, it can indeed start to plateau off earlier. This is the biggest motivation behind the lockdowns imposed as a preventive measure in various places throughout the world.

To be sure, in the very long term, the infection will get to everyone - just like the common cold or the flu or seasonal viral fevers. The idea is to slow down the current rate of growth to a level that the healthcare system can actually handle. Since the Indian public healthcare system is not as well funded or furnished as in some other places, it is prudent to impose preventive measures earlier on than in other places.

    Daily growth rate and Compounded daily growth rate of number of COVID19 cases in India (Indian nationals only) starting March 5

      The figure below shows:
      1. The daily percentage growth - from one day to the next, in blue bars.
      2. The compounded daily growth rate from the starting point till each day, in the red line.
      Since the daily rate can fluctuate (sometimes significantly), the compounded rate can be helpful in giving a better overall picture of the situation. This compounded daily growth rate for a given day is calculated as follows:
        Ni = N0(1 + Ri)i
      • i is the number of days since the starting point in this analysis (March 5)
      • Ri is the compounded daily growth rate from N0 till Ni
      • Ni is the Number of cases on the ith day
      • N0 is the Number of cases on the 0th day (i.e. on March 5)



  • The chart show the growth rate increasing significantly in the initial days, and then fluctuating for the next days, and it appears to fall into a stable pattern in the last few days.
  • The drastic fluctuations in the daily growth rate illustrate the significance of looking at the daily compounded growth rate.

  • The compounded daily growth rate appears to become stable at around 20% over the last few days.
  • This does not mean it will stay stable.
  • Even if it does stay stable around the 20% mark, a daily compounded growth rate of around 20% can very quickly lead to a bad situation - with numbers doubling every four days
